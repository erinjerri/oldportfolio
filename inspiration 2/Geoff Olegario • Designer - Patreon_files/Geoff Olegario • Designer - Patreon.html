Patreon

Time to make some bigger boy decisions. My lessons as a lone designer.

Patreon was my first position as a lone designer. Definitely an exciting adventure. It was my chance to shape an interesting product and make some big boy decisions. And I came out learning some new lessons and explored my craft a little more.

Patreon's problem: How can this new form of crowdfunding help artists and content creators become independent and support craft? Yes, there is YouTube and Kickstarter and Indiegogo. But what sets Patreon apart is that it focuses on the community of artists and fans, not ads and ad revenue or the pressure of having a giant concept of an idea that needs to be funded to manifest. Patreon offers a new way artists can spread their craft, improve their craft and have fans fund their craft.

We began with a redesign of the core experience, consuming content and converting users to become supporters of creators.

This was the original creation view page. It's quite a lot to look at. I found it to be very noisey with a lot of elements that competed with one another, so I began by talking with the founders to find out what we wanted from this page.

The two top goals were consumption of content and conversion of user to patrons. So that's what my solution showed brought. This is an example of a page that the vast majority of users land on and choose to convert, so I clearly wanted to dilute the page to its most minimal appealing elements, the content and the big button to become a patron. This is my finished product:

This finished page laid the foundation for the rest of the Patreon product, beginning the dilution of visual noise. We wanted the users and their creations to shine and the actions for users to be as clear as possible, so we kept a very minimal look. Not only did I minimalize the visual design, but also kept in mind the mobile framework to make design decisions. I modularized each section to also make sense in a mobile framework. I also designed the V1 of their iOS app.
